The Why

Why do PMs spend 6 hours on work that should take minutes?

Before touching any design tool, I interviewed two product managers, both running 2-week sprints, both spending their Monday mornings manually converting Figma screens into Jira tickets before sprint planning could even start.

The picture was the same both times: a PM with Figma, Jira, and a blank Notion doc open simultaneously, spending 4–5 hours on work that required no creative judgment but carried full accountability when it was wrong.

Why spend the most valuable hours of the sprint on formatting, when that time belongs to strategy?

Concept

From word association to wireframe to visual direction

Before designing any screen, I defined what SprintMind should feel like. Starting with word and concept association, four brand attributes emerged: Intelligent Speed, Trustworthy Precision, Invisible AI, and Bridge Builder. I then explored four visual directions — testing how each attribute translated into a real visual language. The combination of Warm Editorial and Holographic Light won: human enough to feel trusted, structural enough to feel intelligent. Design

Token system locked. Then prototype. Then iterate.

With the visual direction confirmed, I locked the design token system before building any screen — color, type, spacing, semantic states all defined first. The prototype followed a deliberate tool progression: first flows built in Claude Artifacts, interactive prototype in Lovable, final high-fidelity design in Figma Make. Each tool had a specific job, and each handoff was a forcing function to refine what wasn't working.

Testing

Three rounds. Two pivots. One insight that changed everything.

Round 1 surfaced that the 7-step flow was too heavy — I was designing for completeness when the goal was speed. Collapsed to 4 steps. Round 2 asked the question I had no answer to: "What does the AI do when it doesn't know something?" That question became the flag system — the most important design decision in the product. Round 3 was a user testing session that restructured how the landing page proved its own claim.

The Turning Point

I was optimizing
for the wrong moment.

I spent weeks perfecting the generation screen — the dramatic moment where the AI produces a full backlog in under 2 minutes. It's impressive. A PM sees the tickets appear and says "wow."

But they say "wow" once.

What makes them come back — what makes this trustworthy enough to use on a real sprint — is what the AI does when it doesn't know something. The confidence score that blocks generation at 41%. The critical flag on a ticket that surfaces a missing loading state before the sprint starts. The export gate that stays locked until what matters is resolved. The wow moment gets the demo. The flags get the trust.

"Trust isn't built by the AI being right. It's built by the AI being honest about where it isn't sure. Designing 'I don't know' states turned out to be harder — and more important — than designing the generation itself."

— The insight that reframed everything

Building with AI

Iterative prompting
changed how I design.

Figma Make was the build environment. But the real skill wasn't using it — it was learning how to talk to it.

01

Lead with constraints, not intent

The most reliable prompts start with what NOT to change. "Don't touch the nav. Only update the confidence bar." AI tools are eager — they'll refactor everything unless you anchor what's working.

Surgical prompting
02

Describe the delta, not the target

Instead of "make a flag card like X," write "the current card has no icon — add a 🚩 left and two buttons right: Fill in now (violet) and Skip (ghost)." Change descriptions beat destination descriptions.

Delta description
03

Separate visual from behavior

Visual prompts (layout, color, spacing) and logic prompts (states, interactions) need to be separate. Mixing them produces half-right output every time.

Prompt separation
04

Iterating on what's built is harder than building

Once you have a working frame, surgical changes without breaking what works becomes the real skill. AI doesn't have your visual memory — each prompt is a fresh negotiation.

Preservation-first iteration

Key Learnings

What I'd tell myself
on day one.

Design the uncertainty, not just the answer

The most important screens in an AI product are where the AI doesn't know what to do. Designing "I don't know" states is harder and more valuable than designing the output.

Surgical prompts beat complete rewrites

Anchor what works before describing the change. "Don't touch X, only change Y" is the most powerful sentence in AI-assisted design.

Two interviews changed the questions I was asking

Talking to PMs before opening any design tool changed everything downstream — what the problem actually was, who the user was, and what success looked like. Research isn't a phase. It's the foundation the whole thing sits on.
