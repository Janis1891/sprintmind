The Why

Why do PMs spend 6 hours on work that should take minutes?

Before touching any design tool, I interviewed two PMs — one at a mid-size SaaS team, one at a scale-up. Both confirmed the same picture: every other Monday, Figma open, Jira open, a blank Notion doc open, and 4–5 hours of manual translation work before sprint planning could start.

They weren't slow. They were doing work that a tool should absorb. No product connected design intent to ticket structure. Jira doesn't read Figma. Figma doesn't write tickets. The PM was the bridge, every sprint, on their own.

01 / Research

2 interviews. 6 tools.
One gap nobody had closed.

I started with two structured PM interviews. Both confirmed the same pattern: 4–5 hours per sprint lost to translation work, no reliable method for complete ticket decomposition, and context leaking between every tool.

From there I benchmarked 6 tools — sprint planning, ticket generation, and design handoff. None connected design signals to engineering scope together. The interviews confirmed the pain. The benchmark confirmed the gap. SprintMind was the only possible answer to both.

02 / The Design Challenge

How do you build an AI tool
that PMs actually trust?

Not one that generates output. Not one that looks impressive in a demo. One that a PM will use at 6pm on a Thursday when a sprint is tight and the stakes are real.

"Trust isn't built by the AI being right. It's built by the AI being honest about where it isn't sure. Designing I don't know states turned out to be harder — and more important — than designing the generation itself."

— The insight that reframed the entire product
03 / Design Process

Concept, wireframes,

Concept

From word association to wireframe to visual direction

Before designing any screen, I defined what SprintMind should feel like. Starting with word and concept association, four brand attributes emerged: Intelligent Speed, Trustworthy Precision, Invisible AI, and Bridge Builder. I then explored four visual directions — testing how each attribute translated into a real visual language. The combination of Warm Editorial and Holographic Light won: human enough to feel trusted, structural enough to feel intelligent. The first flows were sketched in Claude Artifacts, then refined into wireframes before any pixel-perfect work began.

Design

Token system locked. Then prototype. Then iterate.

With the visual direction confirmed, I locked the design token system before building any screen — color, type, spacing, semantic states all defined first. The prototype followed a deliberate tool progression: first flows built in Claude Artifacts, interactive prototype in Lovable, final high-fidelity design in Figma Make. Each tool had a specific job, and each handoff was a forcing function to refine what wasn't working.

Claude Artifacts → Lovable → Figma Make
Testing

Three rounds. Two pivots. One insight that changed everything.

Round 1 surfaced that the 7-step flow was too heavy — I was designing for completeness when the goal was speed. Collapsed to 4 steps. Round 2 asked the question I had no answer to: "What does the AI do when it doesn't know something?" That question became the flag system — the most important design decision in the product. Round 3 was a user testing session that restructured how the landing page proved its own claim.

04 / The Key Decision

The flag system wasn't in
the original design.
It was the critique that changed everything.

After the first prototype review, a peer pointed out something I had rationalized away: the AI would sometimes generate a ticket for a screen that had no error state defined. The output would look correct, but it would silently block development.

Every other tool in the benchmark showed a result and stopped. I decided SprintMind should show a result and a confidence score — surfacing the 41% as clearly as the 94%. That honesty is what earns the PM's trust. Hiding uncertainty would have been a design failure disguised as polish.

"The wow moment gets the demo. The flags get the trust. You need both."

— Learned during user testing

06 / How It Was Built

4 weeks.
A different AI tool for each job.

No single AI tool did everything. Claude held the research and writing. ChatGPT proved stronger for wireframe concepts. Midjourney refined the visual tone. Figma Make shipped the prototype once I had a precise prompt and a working vision board.

Week 1

Two PM interviews reset my whole problem definition.

I went in thinking the problem was ticket quality. PMs told me it was time. That single shift changed the product's success metric, the flow, and what "done" meant.

Week 2

Claude structured the research and the product architecture.

Synthesized 2 hours of notes into a clean problem frame and drafted the PRD. That same week it forgot a user's job title twice and invented a competitor that doesn't exist.

Week 3

ChatGPT beat Claude on wireframes. Midjourney set the visual tone.

ChatGPT opened UX directions I hadn't considered. The logo fought me all week. Described the outcome, not the component — that's when it clicked.

Week 4

One precise prompt and a vision board shipped the prototype.

Figma Make jumped in quality the moment I gave it a brand reference and a clear goal. The prototype landed at a high level on the first try.

07 / What I Learned

Six things this build
taught me.

01
Talk to users before opening any design tool.
I thought I understood the problem from the brief. Two 45-minute interviews completely reframed it. The tool was never the constraint — time was. That shift changed the product's success metric, the flow, and what "done" meant.
02
The uncertainty IS the feature.
Every competing tool shows a result and stops. SprintMind shows a result and a confidence score. That honesty — surfacing the 41% as clearly as the 94% — is what earns trust. Hiding uncertainty would have been a design failure disguised as polish.
03
Describe the outcome, not the component.
Prompting with rigid structure specs produced brittle outputs. Describing the feeling, the use case, and the context produced things I could actually build from. This applied to every tool, but Midjourney especially rewarded evocative language.
04
The basics still need watching.
Claude forgot a user's job title twice, invented a competitor, and wrote in German after I'd asked for English. None of that was fatal, but it means reading the output like an editor. Knowing when to trust the first draft and when to rerun the prompt is its own skill.
05
The wow gets attention. The flags get adoption.
Everyone's first reaction to SprintMind is the 2-minute backlog. But PMs who engaged longer pointed to the flag system as the reason they'd actually use it. You need both — the hook and the trust mechanism.
06
Surgical cuts beat full redesigns.
The original 7-step flow had too much sequential decision work. Steps 5 and 6 were really one screen — scope and review belong together. Recognizing that mid-build and making a precise cut instead of redesigning the whole flow is where design judgment earns its keep.

09 / What's Next

The open problem
is already visible.

The review step needs to be streamlined to reduce decision fatigue across a long backlog. That's the right problem to have after a first MVP — it means the core flow works, and the edge is now about depth, not breadth.

The tools compressed the slow parts of the work — the research piles, the first-draft redrafts, the layout scaffolding — freeing my attention for the parts that actually needed a person. Pick the right tool, let it carry what it carries well, and keep the decisions human.
